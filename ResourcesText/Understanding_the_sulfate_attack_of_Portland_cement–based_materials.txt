studied, and the mineralogical alterations were inv estigated by both experiments and thermodynamic mod eling. 19 
When an electric current flows out of the cement pa ste, the electric migration of ions induced sulfate  ingress and 20

1 Introduction 35 
 36 
Cement-based materials are porous and consist of mu ltiple phases. The hydration products (solid phase)  form the 37 

=== Page 4 ===
3 skeleton of the pore structures, and the pore solut ion (aqueous phase) and air (gaseous phase) fill in side the pores. 38 
In a fully hydrated cement paste, the hydration pro ducts are approximately at equilibrium with the por e solution. 39 
However, this state will be affected by the ionic e xchange between the pore solution and the external environment. 40 
Such a process usually induces mineralogical altera tions and is described as a chemical attack. The at tack is 41 
affected by the type, rate, and direction of the io n exchange, as well as the chemical reactions that occur. 42 
 43 
The ingress of external sulfate will cause sulfate attack, which is one of the most widely recognized and 44 
well-studied chemical attacks. In Portland cement s ystems, sulfate attacks are usually characterized b y the 45 
formation of ettringite, and by the expansion, crac king, and strength loss thereafter [1-3]. Recent th eories relate the 46 
expansion to the crystallization pressure caused by  the formation of ettringite from oversaturated sol ution in small 47 
pores [4-6]. The crystals growing in confined pores , e.g., in the C-S-H gel, generate crystallization pressure and 48 
thus expansive stress [7,8], whilst those growing i n unrestrained pore spaces do not. Gypsum usually f orms at the 49 
late stage of sulfate attack. There was once a deba te on whether the formation of gypsum is a cause [9 -11] or an 50 
effect [12,13] of the expansion and cracking. Recen t studies proposed that the presence of gypsum indu ces a high 51 
supersaturation with respect to ettringite, and thu s a high crystallization pressure and more expansio n [2,14,15]. 52 
 53 
The type of cation that coincides with sulfate grea tly influences the degradation process. When the ca tion is Mg 2+ , 54 
surface erosion occurs in addition to expansion [15 -17]. This phenomenon is induced by the formation o f brucite 55 
and magnesium silicate hydrate (M-S-H) [2,16], and is the most important characteristic to distinguish  magnesium 56 
sulfate attack from sodium sulfate attack. It was a lso reported that the co-existence of Na +, K +, and Ca 2+  can reduce 57 
surface erosion and expansion in the MgSO 4 attack [2,16]. When the sulfate is provided by an acidic environment, 58 
neutralization can take place in the pore solution,  and the Ca 2+  can diffuse towards the external environment due t o 59 

=== Page 5 ===
4 the concentration gradient. Under this circumstance , calcium leaching or decalcification will also occ ur together 60 
with the sulfate attack [18-20]. 61 
 62 
In most cases, the ionic exchange between cement-ba sed materials and the external environment is drive n by the 63 
concentration gradient, and this process is known a s diffusion. In certain field conditions, the expos ed surface of a 64 
structure is subjected to strong evaporation condit ions. The moisture gradient between the external en vironment 65 
and cement-based materials induces capillary suctio n, and the ionic species will be transported by adv ection [21]. 66 
Under an applied electric field, the ionic species in the pore solution can also migrate, which is kno wn as electric 67 
migration [22-24]. The transportation of ionic spec iesâ€”their diffusion, advection, and electric migrat ion in 68 
cement-based materials is described by the Nernst-P lanck equation [22-25]. During the ionic transporta tion, 69 
chemical reactions can take place. For example, SO 42- can react with monosulfate to form ettringite [1,2 0,26]. This 70 
reaction impacts not only the mass transport of SO 42- but also the strength of Ca 2+  and AlO 2- by disturbing the 71 
equilibrium between the hydration products and the pore solution. In this respect, the concurrent chem ical reactions 72 
and ionic transportation both play significant role s in the ingress of sulfate. 73 
 74 
In some cases, cement-based materials are directly exposed to applied electric fields, e.g., the DC st ray current that 75 
is present in underground metro tunnels [27-29]. El ectric migration can accelerate the ingress of aggr essive ions 76 
from the external environment and remove the calciu m and alkali metals from the pore solution [30,31].  Electric 77 
fields are also applied in some modern concrete tec hniques, e.g., chloride extraction, realkalinizatio n, cathodic 78 
protection, and electrochemical deposit [32-35]. In  these techniques, certain types of ions are design ed to migrate, 79 
but the migrations of other ions have to be conside red because they may induce side effects [36]. Elec tric migration 80 
is also a useful tool for laboratory acceleration t ests, e.g., the  chloride ion penetration tests (ASTM C1202), calciu m 81 

=== Page 6 ===
5 leaching acceleration tests [37,38], etc. Recent st udies proposed that electric migration tests can be  potentially used 82 
in the acceleration of sulfate attacks [39-41]. How ever, a deeper understanding of the mineralogical a lterations and 83 
the degradation mechanism under such circumstances will be needed to evaluate the validity of this tec hnique. 84 
 85 
In a conventional sulfate attack caused by diffusio n, both the cations and anions in the external solu tion diffuse into 86 
the cement-based materials, while the ions in the p ore solution, e.g. Ca 2+ , OH -, and alkali metal ions diffuse 87 
outward. In contrast, under applied electric fields , the cations and anions migrate in different direc tions, and thus 88 
the degradation behavior and mechanism may differ f rom the diffusion-induced sulfate attacks. This pap er studies 89 
the MgSO 4 and Na 2SO 4 attacks on Portland cement paste in the presence o f applied electric fields. The chemical 90 
and mineralogical alterations during the degradatio n process were analyzed by scanning electron micros copy 91 
(SEM), energy dispersive X-ray spectrometry (EDS), X-ray diffractometry (XRD), and thermogravimetric a nalysis 92 
(TGA). The results suggest different degradation be haviors between the areas near the cathode and anod e, and 93 
between the specimens exposed to different sulfate sources. To better understand the degradation mecha nism, an 94 
approach was proposed to simplify the electric migr ation processes, and changes of the phase assemblag es during 95 
the simplified electric migration processes were th en calculated by thermodynamic modeling. By combini ng the 96 
pore solution chemistry obtained in the thermodynam ic modeling with the Nernst-Planck equation, the mi gration 97 
behaviors of various ionic species were analyzed to  explain the different degradation behaviors observ ed 98 
experimentally. 99

4.1 Comparison between test results and thermodynam ic modeling 526 
 527 
The mineralogical alterations that occurred near th e cathode agreed well with the thermodynamic modeli ng, as 528 
indicated by the arrows in Fig. 13a. After exposure  to MgSO 4 for 28 d, CaSO 4 was observed to form in the outer 529 

=== Page 30 ===

comparison, the specimen exposed to Na 2SO 4 for 28 d remained at an earlier degradation stage,  when CaSO 4 did 535

of S that has been transported from the cathodic so lution into the cement paste (EDS, cf. Fig. 3b and Fig. 8b). 537

The mineralogical alterations occurred in a shallow  depth (~0.1 mm) in the cement paste near the anode , but had 539 
proceeded to the last degradation stage in both exp osure conditions when comparing the test results wi th the 540 
thermodynamic modeling (Fig. 13c and 13e). For the MgSO 4 exposure, the presence of Mg-containing products 541 
(brucite and M-S-H, SEM, Fig. 5) agrees with the th ermodynamic calculation, while for the Na 2SO 4 exposure, the 542 
Na-rich silica gel (Fig. 10) coincides with the Na uptake by C-S-H in the thermodynamic modeling. A Ca SO 4-rich 543

4.2 Migration behavior of Mg 2+ , Na +, and SO 42- 547 
 548 
The degradation near the cathode and anode proceede d in different ways. Near the cathode, the anions f rom the 549 
external solution (mainly SO 42-) migrated into a deep area, and the mineralogical alteration proceeded gradually 550 
following the prediction of thermodynamic modeling.  For the anode area exposed to MgSO 4, the incorporation of 551 

=== Page 31 ===
30 Mg 2+  occurred in a shallow area with the mineralogical alteration proceeding directly to the late degradat ion stage. 552 
In contrast, Na + could penetrate deep into the cement paste, but th e mineralogical alteration occurred also in a 553 
shallow area. The different migration behaviors of the ionic species can be explained by the relative flux which is 554 
obtained from the solution chemistry calculated by thermodynamic modeling. 555 
 556 
According to section 3.3.2, an ionâ€™s capability for  migration under electric fields depends on its rel ative flux, while 557 
its accumulation rate in a degradation area depends  on the relative flux of the other ion that is bein g replaced. For 558 
the degradation process near the cathode, the high relative flux of OH - suggests a fast accumulation of SO 42- at an 559 
early degradation stage, whilst the increased relat ive flux of SO 42- indicates an accelerated migration rate towards 560 
the inner part of the specimen (Fig. 14a). In contr ast, for the degradation process near the anode, th e smaller 561 
relative flux of Ca 2+  than that of OH - suggests a slower accumulation rate of the cations  (Mg 2+  or Na +) than the 562 
outflow of OH - (Fig. 14b and 14c), which coincides with the shall ow depth of Mg 2+  and Na + incorporation (Fig. 5 563 
and 10). The strengths of Na + and Mg 2+  in the pore solution (Fig. 13d and 13f) and thus t heir relative fluxes (Fig. 564 
14b and 14c) were different after the chemical reac tions. As a result, Na + and Mg 2+  showed different migration 565 
behaviors (also compare Fig. 3 and Fig. 8). The dif ferent migration behaviors of SO 42- and Mg 2+  were once realized 566 
when using thermodynamic modeling to study the magn esium sulfate attack simply caused by diffusion [15 ,16]. It 567 
was reported that the penetration depth of Mg 2+  is usually overestimated compared to that of SO 42-. According to 568 
the findings in this paper, this can be explained b y the low concentration of Mg 2+  in the pore solution and thus its 569 
slower diffusion rate than SO 42-. 570 
 571 
The electric migration of the cations and anions ca n also influence each other because both processes carry electric 572 
charges. The experiment was conducted under a const ant electric current. The same amount of charges sh ould pass 573 

=== Page 32 ===
31 through the specimen during the same period of time , no matter the specimen was exposed to MgSO 4 or Na 2SO 4. In 574 
the cathode side, SO 42- was migrated from the cathodic solution into the c ement paste. It penetrated through the 575 
specimen together with OH - (and other anions) in the pore solution. In the me anwhile, the Ca 2+  in the pore solution 576 
were released into the cathodic solution, but this process carried less electric charges due to the lo w relative flux of 577 
Ca 2+  than the anions. In the anode side exposed to MgSO 4, the migration of Mg 2+  and Ca 2+  towards the inner part of 578 
the specimen were at low rates. The electric charge s were mainly carried by the OH - migrating from the inner part 579 
of the specimen towards the anode side, and finally  into the anodic solution. In contrast, in the case  of Na 2SO 4 580 
exposure, Na + could penetrate through the specimen much more eas ily than Mg 2+ , and its electric migration can 581 
carry much more electric charges. Under such circum stance, the electric migration of anions (SO 42- and OH -) will 582 
carry fewer charges, and thus the sulfate ingress w ill proceed on a lower rate compared with the situa tion of MgSO 4 583 
exposure. Similar results were also reported in [41 ] but no convincing explanations were ever given. 584

4.4 Comparison with coupled reactive-transport mode ling 646 
 647 
As stated in [1], the coupled reactive-transport mo deling and the pure thermodynamic modeling presente d 648 
comparable results in revealing the phase alteratio ns of a diffusion-induced sulfate attack. The pure thermodynamic 649 
modeling is usually easy to use and provides fast r esults, but the results do not correspond directly with the real 650 
exposure time or the depth from the exposure surfac e. In comparison, coupled reactive-transport modeli ng provides 651 
the information of time and depth, but the accuracy  depends strongly on the preciseness of models (e.g ., 652 
transportation equation, fluid transport, charge ba lance) [21,67] and parameters (e.g., diffusion coef ficient) [1]. This 653 
paper proposed a simplified approach for the pure t hermodynamic modeling study of electric-migration-i nduced 654 
sulfate attacks. Such an approach focuses on the ch emical and mineralogical aspects and clarifies alte rations of the 655 
hydration products. Although the real distribution of the products was not presented, this information  can be 656 
reflected by the relative flux that is proposed her e. The advantages and validity of this approach wil l be further 657 
demonstrated in an upcoming paper investigating the  effect of supplementary cementitious materials in the same 658 
situation. 659 
 660 

=== Page 36 ===
35 The thermodynamic modeling calculation conducted in  this paper is based on the assumption that the kin etics of the 661 
chemical reactions is much faster than the transpor tation of ionic species, and that the hydration sys tem is close to 662 
an equilibrium state. As reviewed in [68], this ass umption is usually valid  in most practical cases involving the 663 
diffusion of ions in saturated systems, and thus it  is widely accepted by the existing pure thermodyna mic modeling 664 
studies [1,2,11,14-16]. In contrast, electric migra tion usually induces a quicker ionic motion, and th us the 665 
equilibrium state is more difficult to be reached [ 24]. When studying the electric migration of chlori de by coupled 666 
reactive-transport models, an equilibrium state was  assumed in [69] and the test results agree well wi th the 667 
modeling. The results of this paper also show that the migration behavior of ionic species coincides w ith the 668 
solution chemistry (relative flux) under the assump tion of equilibrium. It can be thus inferred that t he real solution 669 
chemistry in the hydrated cement paste is similar t o that under the equilibrium state and that the equ ilibrium 670 
assumption is likely to be valid in the case of ele ctric migration. 671 
 672 
The electric migration behavior of ionic species ca n be represented by the relative flux, which depend s on the ionâ€™s 673 
diffusion coefficient and concentration in the pore  solution. The diffusion coefficients of different ions are usually 674 
at the same order of magnitude, as shown in Table 2 . However, their concentrations can span several or ders of 675 
magnitude, which induces wide differences in the re lative fluxes and is responsible for the different migration 676 
behavior of Mg 2+ , Na +, and SO 42- under applied electric fields. In this respect, fo r a reactive-transport process, it is 677 
sometimes acceptable to suppose the same diffusion coefficient for different ionic species, as was don e in 678 
[16,70,71]. Nevertheless, the effect of chemical re actions on the concentration and thus the migration  of ionic 679 
species should be carefully considered. 680

simultaneously. In contrast, in the case of DC stra y current, these two processes occur separately. Th e electric 685

However, thermodynamic modeling predicted an increa sed volume of hydration products near the cathode, which 695 
may yet cause expansion. Based on the results of th is paper, it is difficult to determine whether expa nsion and 696
